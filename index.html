01 — Visual Hierarchy

The Eye Always
Follows Rules

Visual hierarchy controls the order in which the eye reads information. You control this with size, contrast, weight, color, and spacing.

👆 When all elements are similar size, nothing stands out. Strong contrast in scale creates a clear reading order.

01

Size & Scale

Bigger = more important. Use a 3-step scale: hero text → sub text → body. Never use more than 3 type sizes in one design.

02

Color & Contrast

High contrast draws the eye first. Your CTA button must be the highest-contrast element on the canvas.

03

Weight & Boldness

Bold text commands attention before regular text. When everything is bold, nothing is bold. Use weight sparingly.

04

Position & Flow

Viewers read top-left to bottom-right. Place your most important information top-left or center-top.

05

Spacing & Isolation

Surround an element with space and it becomes more important. Crowded = cheap. Spacious = premium.

06

Color as Accent

Use your brand accent color on the single most important element. If everything is colored, nothing stands out.

✓ DO

Use 3 clear levels: a dominant headline (60–80px on social), supporting subtext (18–22px), and small body (14–16px). Maintain at least 2× size difference between levels.

✗ DON'T

Make everything the same size to "feel balanced." Equal-sized elements create visual noise. The viewer doesn't know where to look first, so they look nowhere.

"Design is not just what it looks like and feels like. Design is how it works."— Steve Jobs
02 — Rules of Good Design

The Principles Every
Good Design Follows

C.A.R.P. + Balance + Unity underpin virtually every successful visual design — from billboards to Instagram posts.

C

Contrast

If two elements aren't the same, make them very different. Contrast creates visual interest, guides attention, and communicates importance.

↳ Contrast in action — AA/AAA accessibility standards

✓ DO

Use a dark background with a bright accent. Make your CTA dramatically different from the background — not a subtle shade variation.

✗ DON'T

Light grey text on white, or yellow text on cream — these fail accessibility and look amateurish.

A

Alignment

Every element should have a visual connection to something else. Random placement looks accidental. Deliberate alignment creates order and professionalism.

↳ Alignment demonstration
✗ NO ALIGNMENT
✓ LEFT-ALIGNED
  • Pick one alignment axis and stick to it throughout the design
  • Use an invisible grid — divide your canvas into equal columns
  • Edges of text, images, and shapes should line up with at least one other element
  • Never center-align long paragraphs — it's hard to read
R

Repetition

Repeat visual elements throughout a design. Same colors, fonts, shapes, and spacing patterns create cohesion and brand recognition.

  • Define 2–3 brand colors and use them consistently across all posts
  • Use the same 2 fonts in the same roles (display + body) in every design
  • Create templates for your most common post types and never deviate
  • Repeat shape language: if your brand uses rounded corners, use them everywhere
P

Proximity

Group related items together. Items close to each other are perceived as related. Use space to communicate relationships between elements.

↳ White Space — Toggle between crowded and breathable layouts

  • Use 2–3× more space between groups than within a group

B

Balance

Balance doesn't mean symmetry. It means visual weight feels stable. Asymmetric balance feels more dynamic and modern than centered layouts.

Symmetrical
Classic, formal, stable
Asymmetrical
Dynamic, modern, tension
Radial
Focus, energy, logos
U

Unity

Unity means the design feels like one cohesive piece. Every element should belong. If you can remove something without harming the design, remove it.

  • Limit your palette to 3 colors: 1 dominant, 1 secondary, 1 accent
  • Use only 2 fonts maximum per design system
  • All photos should have a consistent treatment (same filter, same crop ratio)
  • When in doubt, remove it. Simplicity serves unity better than complexity.
"Perfection is achieved not when there is nothing more to add, but when there is nothing left to take away."— Antoine de Saint-Exupéry

Where the Eye
Wants to Go

1/3

Horizon Rule

Place the horizon at the 1/3 or 2/3 mark, never in the center. A centered horizon feels static. Off-center creates energy.

2/3

Subject Placement

For portraits and product shots, place them on a vertical third line with eyes on the upper horizontal third. Leave space in the direction they face.

⅓

Text Zones

On social posts with text overlay, keep text in the bottom or top third. The middle third is the "dead zone" for text — it competes with your subject.

04 — Psychology of Design

Design is a
Conversation with the Brain

Every design decision triggers a psychological response. Understanding these responses lets you design with intention — not luck.

↑

Anchoring Effect

The first number a viewer sees anchors their perception. Show the original price before the sale price. First impression sets the reference point.

⬜

Hick's Law

More choices = more time to decide = more likely to choose nothing. Limit your CTA to one action per design. One message = one action.

👁

Face Tracking

Human brains are hard-wired to look at faces. If you use a person's photo, viewers follow where the face is looking — use this to direct attention.

🧠

Cognitive Load

The brain has limited processing power. Complex, cluttered designs cause mental fatigue and abandonment. Every extra element forces the viewer to work harder.

🔁

Mere Exposure Effect

People prefer things they've seen before. Consistent branding across posts builds familiarity. Familiarity builds trust. Trust builds purchase intent.

↗

Loss Aversion

People are 2× more motivated by avoiding loss than gaining a reward. "Don't miss out" outperforms "Get a reward." Frame copy around what they'll lose.

↕

Line Height (Leading)

Body text: 1.5–1.65× the font size. Headlines: 1.0–1.15×. 16px body text needs 24–26px line height to breathe properly.

↔

Letter Spacing

Large display text (60px+): slightly negative tracking (–1 to –2px). All-caps labels: positive tracking (+2 to +4px) improves readability.

≡

Text Alignment

Left-align body copy always. Center-align only for headlines and short captions. Never justify text — it creates rivers of white space.

07 — Gestalt Principles

How the Brain
Groups What It Sees

Gestalt principles explain how humans naturally perceive groups, patterns, and wholes from individual elements.

Proximity: Elements close together are perceived as a group. Watch how the dots form 3 distinct groups simply through spacing — no borders, no colors needed. Use this in social posts to visually group your headline with its subtext, and your CTA with its supporting context.

Figure-Ground

The eye separates foreground from background. Use contrast to define what's the "figure."

Common Region

Elements inside a boundary are seen as a group. Borders and cards group content without labels.

Closure

The brain completes incomplete shapes. Use this for logos and creative cropping at frame edges.

↳ Applied to social media layouts
  • Proximity: Keep your post's CTA and price in the same visual zone — they're related, so they should be close
  • Similarity: Use the same font treatment for all product names across a carousel — viewers recognise the pattern
  • Continuity: A diagonal composition guides the eye from the image to the headline to the CTA
  • Closure: Crop your product photo at the edge of the frame — the brain completes the object, creating depth

01

Too Many Fonts

Using 4–5 different typefaces in one design. When combined, they create visual chaos and destroy brand cohesion. The rule: 2 font families maximum per design, 3 weights maximum.

Fix: Choose 1 display font + 1 body font and commit
02

Rainbow Color Palettes

Using every brand color in every design. Six colors in one post creates visual noise and makes nothing stand out. Brand guide might have 6 colors — that doesn't mean use all 6 everywhere.

Fix: Max 3 colors per design, following the 60-30-10 rule
03

Stretching Photos & Logos

Distorting images to fit a space by changing the aspect ratio. This is one of the most immediately visible amateur mistakes. A stretched logo tells a client you don't respect their brand.

Fix: Lock aspect ratio. Design the crop intentionally. Never stretch.
04

No Breathing Room (Crowding)

Filling every inch of the canvas because "we have a lot to say." White space is not wasted space — it's a design element. Crowded designs feel cheap and hard to read.

Fix: Add at least 40–60px of padding on all sides. When in doubt, remove an element.
05

All-Caps Body Text

Writing entire paragraphs in ALL CAPS because it "looks impactful." All-caps text is 15–20% harder to read. The brain reads word shapes, and all-caps destroys those shapes.

Fix: Reserve ALL CAPS for labels and short tags (max 4–5 words) only
06

Centered Everything

Center-aligning every element "because it feels balanced." Center alignment is the most overused layout choice. It often creates weak, passive compositions that lack direction.

Fix: Default to left-alignment. Use center only for headlines and short standalone quotes.
07

Invisible Text on Busy Backgrounds

Placing text directly on a detailed image without a scrim or overlay — making the text unreadable. Readability always wins over aesthetics.

Fix: Dark overlay (40–60% opacity), blur backdrop, or solid card behind text
08

Multiple CTAs on One Post

"Like, Follow, Share, Visit, DM, Comment" — five CTAs means no single action feels urgent. Every CTA you add reduces the chance of any CTA converting. One ask per post.

Fix: One CTA per design. Make it the highest-contrast element on the canvas.
09

Raster Logos at Small Sizes

Using JPEG/PNG logos at small sizes. JPEGs lose quality and can't be scaled up. If you're placing a logo, always use the SVG or vector version.

Fix: Always ask clients for SVG, AI, or EPS files. Never accept only a JPEG logo.
10

Low-Resolution Images

Using small images at large sizes, causing visible pixelation. A blurry hero image instantly kills trust and perception of quality — especially on Retina screens.

Fix: Social media minimum 1080×1080px. Print minimum 300dpi.
09 — Quick Reference

The Cheat Sheet
You'll Actually Use

Bookmark this. These are the numbers, ratios, and rules that make design decisions faster and more confident.

TYPOGRAPHY SIZES

  • Instagram display: 60–80px
  • Headline: 32–48px
  • Subhead: 20–24px
  • Body: 16–18px
  • Caption/label: 11–13px
  • Line height: 1.5–1.65×

SPACING RULES

  • Social post padding: 48–80px
  • Between sections: 40–60px
  • Within a group: 8–16px
  • Between groups: 32–48px
  • Story padding: 60px sides
  • Safe zone: 10% from edges

SOCIAL SIZES (PX)

  • IG Square: 1080 × 1080
  • IG Portrait: 1080 × 1350
  • IG Story/Reel: 1080 × 1920
  • LinkedIn Post: 1200 × 627
  • Twitter/X: 1600 × 900
  • Facebook Ad: 1200 × 628

COLOR RULES

  • 60-30-10 color ratio
  • Max 3 colors per design
  • Min contrast: 4.5:1 for text
  • Accent on CTA only
  • Test in greyscale first
  • Never pure #000000 black

FONT RULES

  • Max 2 font families
  • Max 3 font weights
  • Never stretch fonts
  • Pair serif + sans-serif
  • Embed fonts before sharing
  • Never Comic Sans / Papyrus

IMAGE RULES

  • Always use vector logos (SVG)
  • Social: minimum 72dpi
  • Print: minimum 300dpi
  • Crop, don't stretch
  • Scrim text overlays (40–60%)
  • Consistent treatment per carousel

HIERARCHY ORDER

  • 1st — Size (biggest = first)
  • 2nd — Contrast
  • 3rd — Weight (Bold)
  • 4th — Color
  • 5th — Position (top-left)
  • 6th — Space (isolation)

PRE-EXPORT CHECKLIST

  • ☐ Spell-check everything
  • ☐ Check logo is vector
  • ☐ Verify text contrast ratios
  • ☐ Check consistent spacing
  • ☐ Preview at display size
  • ☐ View in greyscale
